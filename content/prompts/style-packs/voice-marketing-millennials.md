# Writing Style Guide: The Marketing Millennials (Daniel Murray)

**Core principle:** No-BS peer talk. Smart but never stuffy. Practical. Relatable.

---

## Voice & Tone
- Casual and conversational — like texting a fellow marketer
- Authentic, unfiltered, no corporate speak ever
- Peer-to-peer, not guru-to-student
- Accessible expertise — smart without being intimidating
- Contemporary and platform-aware — references current trends, tactics, and tools
- Personality-driven — the voice is always present, never invisible

---

## Post Structure

```
[Punchy observation or hot take — often a single line, sometimes an emoji]
[Quick context — why this matters right now]
[Tactical breakdown — usually a short list]
[Honest take or contrarian wrap-up]
[Casual CTA or open question to spark replies]
```

---

## How to Open Posts
- Lead with a short, sharp opinion or observation
- "Hot take:", "Unpopular opinion:", or just drop straight into the take
- Emojis used sparingly but intentionally — not decoratively
- The opener should feel like something a smart colleague just said to you in Slack

**Examples of opening energy:**
- "Hot take: most brands aren't losing to competitors."
- "The reason your content isn't converting has nothing to do with the content."
- "Nobody talks about this enough:"
- "Full-funnel or no funnel."

---

## Tactical Lists
- Arrow bullets (→) are common
- Keep each item tight — one line max
- 4-6 items is the sweet spot
- No corporate padding — every item earns its place
- Frame as observations or findings, not rules

---

## Vocabulary
- Colloquial and contemporary: "vibes," "fire," "ngl," "lowkey" — but used with restraint
- Marketing-native terms used naturally: UGC, CTV, ROAS, algorithm, creative fatigue
- No jargon without context
- Emoji used as punctuation or emphasis, not decoration
- Contractions everywhere — never "do not" when "don't" works
- Question marks and exclamations used naturally, never performatively

---

## Rhetorical Patterns
- **The hot take setup** — state a position that feels slightly surprising
- **The "nobody talks about" frame** — positions the writer as surfacing something overlooked
- **Platform specificity** — references actual platforms, formats, features by name
- **The honest admission** — acknowledges complexity or nuance rather than false certainty
- **The open question close** — invites the audience to respond rather than demanding a like

---

## Recurring Topics & Themes
- Marketing operations and what actually moves the needle
- Content strategy — what's working, what's dying
- Platform algorithm changes and how to adapt
- Creative strategy — UGC, native content, format diversity
- B2B marketing and LinkedIn growth
- Industry hot takes and trend analysis

---

## Call to Action Style
- End with a question that invites conversation: "What's the biggest [X] you're seeing right now?"
- Soft and curious — not demanding
- Sometimes no explicit CTA — the post just ends on a strong line

---

## Example Post (Agency Context)

```
Hot take: most brands aren't losing to competitors.

They're losing to their own landing pages.

You can run the best ad in the world and send it to a page that converts at 1.2%.

Here's what we keep seeing kill performance:
→ No clear headline above the fold
→ CTAs that say "Learn More" instead of what happens next
→ Mobile load times over 3 seconds
→ Generic social proof with no specificity

The creative didn't fail. The funnel did.

What's the biggest conversion bottleneck you're seeing right now?
```

---

## Platform Notes
- **LinkedIn**: Native long-form text posts with list formatting; conversational sign-off
- **Instagram**: Carousel format works well — each slide = one tip or insight
- **YouTube Shorts / TikTok**: "Bathroom Break" format — short, punchy, single idea, fast delivery
- **Newsletter**: Sun/Tues/Thurs cadence; GIF headers; multimedia blend
