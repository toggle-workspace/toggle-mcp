# Writing Style Guide: Scott Galloway (No Mercy / No Malice)

**Core principle:** Intellectual provocation with moral urgency. The professor who swears.

---

## Voice & Tone
- Provocative and intellectually confident
- Truth-teller willing to challenge conventional wisdom
- Blends academic rigor with accessible, urgent commentary
- Clinical analysis paired with emotional and moral urgency
- Systems-level thinker — always connects individual topics to broader forces
- Not afraid to be personal, or to be moral

---

## Post Structure

```
[Bold assertion or vivid historical/cultural observation — the thesis]
[Systems-level context — zoom out, connect to something bigger]
[Application to the specific topic]
[Paradox or inversion that reframes the whole thing]
[Uncomfortable implication or call to action]
```

---

## How to Open Posts
- Lead with a thesis that sounds almost too confident
- Use historical observations, cultural patterns, or economic facts to immediately establish stakes
- The opening should signal: "something important is about to be said"

**Examples of opening energy:**
- "Great societies decline when old men chop down forests."
- "The most dangerous lie in marketing is 'we need more awareness.'"
- Start with a provocative framing, then narrow to the specific argument

---

## Rhetorical Devices
- **Paradox & inversion** — inverting expected wisdom to reveal a deeper truth
- **Juxtaposition** — contrasting past optimism with present decline, expectation vs. reality
- **Metaphor** — extended metaphors for complex dynamics (decay, infrastructure, fire)
- **Direct address** — speaking frankly about personal struggle and cultural patterns at once
- **Systems framing** — capitalism, incentive structures, infrastructure, historical forces
- **Moral urgency** — the "and this matters because..." is always present even if unstated

---

## Sentence Patterns
- Short, punchy sentences for rhetorical emphasis
- Longer analytical sentences to build the argument
- Rhythm alternates between expository and declarative
- Clinical language paired with unexpected blunt directness
- References to data and facts embedded naturally, not performatively

---

## Vocabulary
- Academic vocabulary used accessibly — never to show off, always to be precise
- Mixes formal analysis with blunt, colloquial punches
- Economic and systemic language: "incentives," "capital," "infrastructure," "capture"
- Moral language used unselfconsciously: "decline," "accountability," "cowardice"
- Not afraid to be provocative with word choice where a softer synonym would dilute the point

---

## Recurring Themes
- Accountability and the absence of consequences for the powerful
- Technological disruption and its human cost
- Inequality — economic, generational, structural
- Institutional decline and the failure of leadership
- The tension between being "right" and being "effective"
- Resistance to comfortable narratives

---

## Example Post (Agency Context)

```
The most dangerous lie in marketing is "we need more awareness."

Brands have been using that phrase to justify wasted spend for 20 years.
It's how mediocre agencies avoid accountability.

Awareness doesn't pay payroll. Pipeline does.

The companies winning right now aren't the loudest ones.
They're the ones who built a direct line between content and revenue —
and had the discipline to measure it.

That's not a creative problem. It's an accountability problem.

The agencies that survive the next decade will be the ones who stop selling services
and start selling outcomes.
```

---

## Platform Notes
- **LinkedIn**: Long-form works well; analytical breakdown posts perform strongly
- **Instagram**: Best for punchy thesis + short argument; quote-card format fits the style
- **YouTube Shorts**: Works as a "hot take" format — bold opening claim, 2-3 supporting points, sharp close
