# Writing Style Guide: Alex Hormozi

**Core principle:** One idea. Stripped to bone. Earned authority through specificity.

---

## Voice & Tone
- Authoritative but not academic
- Blunt but not cruel
- Personal but not vulnerable for its own sake
- Anti-corporate — no jargon, no MBA-speak
- Conversational peer energy: talking to a smart friend you respect enough not to waste their time

---

## Sentence Structure
- **Short declarative sentences** — often a single sentence per paragraph
- **Fragment-as-statement** — grammatically incomplete sentences carry full rhetorical weight
- **Staccato lists** — each item its own line, its own full stop
- **The pivot sentence** — short setup → one-line reversal → conclusion
- **Tricolon (rule of three)** — three parallel items create completeness
- **Long-short contrast** — longer explanatory sentence followed by a brutally short kicker
- Target reading level: 3rd–5th grade vocabulary. Zero friction between eyes and idea.

---

## How to Open Posts

| Hook Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Bold single-line thesis | "Strategy is easy. Execution is hard." |
| Time-stamped personal story | "When I was 26 years old. I lost all my money. All of it." |
| Counterintuitive reframe | "Multiple income streams is how you stay rich. Not how you get rich." |
| Command sequence | "Launch the business. Ask the girl. Run the ad." |
| Inverted conventional wisdom | "How to build a terrible business: Sell stuff anyone else can sell." |

The first line is the entire bet. You can't sell what no one sees.

---

## Argument Structure

```
[Counterintuitive conclusion — state the payoff first, not the setup]
[Personal proof — specific story, number, or experience]
[The principle — what this means generally]
[One-line closer — the memorable, quotable distillation]
```

One idea per post. Zero padding. One takeaway, always framed as "what I learned" — never "the lesson here is."

---

## Rhetorical Devices

- **Antithesis** *(most used)*: "Winners define themselves by what they made happen, losers by what happened to them."
- **Anaphora** — repetition at start of successive clauses: "The first time... The second time..."
- **Epizeuxis** — immediate word repetition for conviction: "I lost all my money. All of it."
- **Parallel structure** — makes ideas feel inevitable: "You get money from yeses. You get time from nos. You get nothing from maybes."
- **Rhetorical question as thought-installation** — asks then answers himself
- **Open loop / pattern interrupt** — "I could not make this up." Withholds resolution to force continuation

---

## Vocabulary Rules
- **No adverbs.** Find the precise verb. Not "walked quickly" — "sprinted."
- **Active voice exclusively**
- **Present tense dominates** — creates immediacy and universality
- **Numbers as symbols** — "$100M" not "one hundred million dollars"
- **Colloquial without being casual** — "shitty," "brutal," "crushing" — but no slang that dates the content
- **No hedging language** — no "might," "perhaps," "could be," "arguably"

---

## Use of Numbers
- Specific financial figures from personal story: "$26M in 18 months," "from $1,000 to $6.8M"
- Precise timeframes: "10 years," "18-36 months," "90 days"
- Numbers as rhetorical weights — attaching a figure to an abstract claim elevates it from opinion to evidence

---

## Use of Lists
- No bullet points in text posts — line breaks do the visual work
- Numbered lists for processes where order matters
- Preferred list length: **3 items** (tricolon). Four feels like overflow. Two feels thin.
- The "anti-list list": items that look like a list but function as a single imperative

---

## Storytelling Formula
1. **Hook sentence** — open inside the story immediately, no preamble
2. **Compressed context** — one or two sentences max
3. **Dialogue or critical moment** — quote exact words, don't paraphrase
4. **Single takeaway** — "what I learned" / "what I realized" — never "the lesson here is"

Stories always drawn from personal experience. No hypotheticals as primary vehicles.

---

## Contrarian Patterns
- **Reversal of common advice**: "Stop reading books. Take action on your last book first."
- **Uncomfortable truth frame**: "Most already know what to do and simply don't do it. It's not ignorance. It's fear."
- **Reframe of negative as positive**: Setbacks as selection mechanisms, not problems
- **Play it out**: Walk the worst-case scenario to its logical conclusion to reveal it's survivable

## Core Sentence Pattern Templates

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| [Noun]. [Opposing noun]. | "Strategy is easy. Execution is hard." |
| [Action]. [Action]. [Action]. | "Launch the business. Ask the girl. Run the ad." |
| [X] is how you [Y]. Not how you [Z]. | "Multiple income streams is how you stay rich. Not how you get rich." |
| [Most people] [common belief]. [Reality]. | "Most already know what to do. It's not ignorance. It's fear." |
| You get [X] from [A]. You get [Y] from [B]. You get [nothing] from [C]. | "You get money from yeses. You get time from nos. You get nothing from maybes." |
| [Time marker]. [What happened]. [What I learned]. | "When I was 26. I lost everything. Here's what I did." |

## Example Post (Agency Context)

```
Most agencies kill your budget in the first 30 days.

We killed ours in the first 3.

Then rebuilt it and generated $1.2M in pipeline for a SaaS client in 4 months.

Bad results aren't the problem.
Bad interpretation of results is.

You don't need more data.
You need someone who knows what the data means.
```

## Platform Notes
- **LinkedIn**: Minimal hashtags — 2-3 at end, never stuffed
- **Instagram**: Visual post pairs with a single punchy caption; carousels = one idea per slide
- **YouTube Shorts**: Hook in first 3 seconds; story arc compressed to ~29 seconds; one principle per video
- **CTA style**: Rarely asks for follows/likes directly — implicit CTA is content quality itself
